        My path into research did not begin in a traditional biomedical setting. I trained first as an aerospace engineer, drawn to physics, design, and the challenge of understanding complex systems. Over time, that interest expanded toward living systems, where the questions felt just as rich and the potential impact even more direct.

        Today, I work at the intersection of engineering, biology, and medicine, building experimental and computational tools that help us image, stimulate, and measure biological function in non-invasive ways.

      subtitle: ''
      text: |-
        I studied aerospace engineering at the Polytechnic University of Madrid, where I first learned to think in terms of modeling, instrumentation, and physical systems. From there, my research path took me through ion mobility and mass spectrometry work at Yale, doctoral research in mechanobiology and cerebrospinal fluid mechanics at UC San Diego, and now postdoctoral work at Caltech focused on biomolecular ultrasound, sonogenetics, and engineering in medicine.

        Looking back, the fields have changed, but the core motivation has stayed surprisingly consistent: I like building tools that make hard-to-measure phenomena more visible and more understandable.

        What excites me most is the possibility of translating rigorous engineering into tools that matter in biology and medicine. I am especially interested in research that combines fundamental science with practical usefulness: systems that can help us probe living tissue, control cellular behavior, or create new ways of diagnosing and treating disease.

        I am also drawn to multidisciplinary environments, where ideas move between fields and where progress often comes from combining perspectives that do not usually sit next to each other. That is a big part of why engineering in medicine feels like the right home for my work.
